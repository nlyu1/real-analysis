4 Measurable functions

We take the measurable sets in \(\mathbb R^n\) to be the Borel sets instead of the full completion, in adherence with Lieb and Loss.

  1. Conditions for measurability on \(\mathbb R^n\):
    • Axler, chapter 3.
  2. Littlewood’s three principles;
    • Rudin, chapter 11.
  3. Iterated integrals.
    • Lieb and Loss, chapter 2; Axler, chapter 5.

Key takeaways:

  1. Semi-continuity suffices for measurability.
  2. \(\sigma\)-finiteness is paramount for exchange of integrals.

Product measures, Fubini

Theorem 4.1 (characterization of product measure) Given two \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces \((\Omega_1, \Sigma_1, \mu_1)\) and \((\Omega_2, \Sigma_2, \mu_2)\) define, for every measurable \(A\in \Sigma_1\times \Sigma_2\):

  1. \(f(y) = \mu_1(A_1(y)) = \mu_1(\{x\in \Omega_1: (x, y)\in A\})\).
  2. \(g(x) = \mu_2(A_2(x)) = \mu_2(\{y\in \Omega_2: (x, y)\in A\})\).
Then \(f\) is \(\Sigma_1\)-measurable, \(g\) is \(\Sigma_2\)-measurable, and \[ (\mu_1\times \mu_2)(A) = \int_{\Omega_2} f(y) d\mu_2 = \int_{\Omega_1} g(x) d\mu_1 \]

Figure 4.1: Visual example.

To prove that \(g\) is \(\Sigma_1\)-measurable, we prove that the set \(\mathcal T\) of all subsets of \(\mathbb R\) whose preimage is \(\Sigma_1\)-measurable generates the measurable sets on \(\mathbb R\).

Proposition 4.1 (finiteness) The product \(\Sigma_1\times \Sigma_2\) of two \(\sigma\)-finite measures is \(\sigma\)-finite.
